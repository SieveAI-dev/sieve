Cron and time zones: a common source of confusion.

By default, cron jobs run in the system's local time zone (as reported by `timedatectl` on systemd systems or `date` on any Unix).

To check the system time zone:
  timedatectl           (Linux)
  systemsetup -gettimezone  (macOS, requires sudo)
  date +%Z              (any Unix)

Common pitfalls:

1. Server in UTC, developer in US/Eastern:
   A job scheduled for "0 9 * * *" runs at 09:00 UTC (4 AM Eastern in winter).
   Always document cron times with their timezone.

2. Daylight Saving Time transitions:
   A job scheduled for 02:30 may run twice or not at all when DST changes the clock.
   Use 03:00 or later for daily jobs to avoid DST ambiguity.

3. Different users' crontabs in different time zones:
   Not possible in standard cron. All crontabs use the system timezone.
   Workaround: use the TZ variable at the top of a user's crontab (some cron implementations support this).

4. Kubernetes CronJobs use UTC by default. The spec.timeZone field (added in Kubernetes 1.25) allows a named timezone.

Reading system time settings is purely informational: `timedatectl`, `date`, `cat /etc/timezone`
